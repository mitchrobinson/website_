About Me

My name is Mitch Robinson and I am currently a student in Mechanical Engineering at UC Berkeley experienced in many topics in the world of engineering, design, and numerical methods. My diverse skillset as an engineer has allowed me to work on some truly interesting projects in my early career.

Using my knowledge I have modeled the physical world in an array of ways using modern engineering techniques. MATLAB and FEA software have allowed me take on challenging problems like determining internal stress characteristics, examining fluid flow, and predicting the motion of 3D objects. To control these physical properties, I have significant experience in Simulink and especially LabVIEW controller design. From data acquisition, to wiring, to building complex control systems, my experience has opened a variety of possibilities for future engineering projects.

I am also a proud athlete and am currently the president and captain of the UC Berkeley Roller Hockey Team. My other sports interests include soccer, golf, and skateboarding.

Automatic Nerf Gun

This is the block diagram or control portion of the LabVIEW code. It consists of state machines that help direct the functionality of the controller to do what the programmer wants.

This is the user interface, or LabVIEW front panel, that displays the tracking data and current state while also taking in user commands.

In the spring of 2015 I worked with fellow engineers to design an automatic Nerf turret. The Nerf gun was able to track an IR signal using a mounted Wii Remote. The positional information was then fed through a control algorithm I developed in LabVIEW that could interpret Wii signals and give voltage outputs. These outputs were fed into positional motors (servomotors) that would point the gun at the target and pull the trigger when it was within a specified range.

Pressure Coefficient Distribution

Above is a plot of the distribution of pressure coefficients for the 0012 airfoil for varying angles of attack. To simulate the circulation of air that develops around a given airfoil , I used vortex panel algorithms to place vortices at given points along the wings surface to enforce the no slip condition. From there, the total effect of these vortices could be solved to obtain the various pressure coefficients that outline how the lift is distributed across the airfoil.
